Time Preferences

Discounting

Let's consider an utility flow $u(t)$ for $t$ in $\mathbb{T}$ of an agent.

  1. The value of one unit of utility in period $t$ in terms of present utility ($t=0$) is $D(t)<1$.
  2. The value of $u(t)$ units of utility in period $t$ in terms of present utility is $D(t)\cdot u(t)$.
  3. We refer to $D(t)$ as the discount factor of the individual.

Time Preferences

Discounting — An Example: Present Value

When $u(t)$ for $t$ in $\mathbb{T}$ is the flow of profits for a firm:

  1. The value of one dollar period $t$ in terms of present value ($t=0$) is $D(t)=\frac{1}{(1+r)^{t}}$, where $r$ is the interest rate.
  2. The value of $u(t)$ dollars in period $t$ in terms of present value (PV) is $D(t)\cdot u(t)=\frac{u(t)}{(1+r)^{t}}$.
  3. $\mathrm{PV}(u_{0},u_{1},u_{2},u_{3},\ldots,u_{t},\ldots)=\sum_{t=0}^{+\infty}\frac{u_t}{(1+r)^{t}}$.

Time Preferences

Discounting

Consider discrete time and an utility flow $u(t)$ for $t$ in $\mathbb{T}$ of an agent. With discounting preferences, an agent evaluates utility flows, $(u(0),u(1),u(2),\ldots)$, accordingly to the discounted value of the flow: $$U(u(0),u(1),u(2),\ldots)=\sum_{t=0}^{+\infty}D(t)\cdot u(t).$$

Here, $D(t)$ is the discount factor of period $t$, and $D(t)\cdot u(t)$ is the present utility (the utility in terms of period $t=0$ utility) of receiving $u(t)$ utils in period $t$. Since we assume agents display impatient, they prefer to receive utility sooner than later, it follows that $0<D(t)<1$.

Time Preferences

Exponential Discounting

Exponential Discounting is the standard discounting model. Under exponential discounting $D(t)=\delta^{t}$ where $0<\delta<1$. $$U(u(0),u(1),u(2),\ldots)=\sum_{t=0}^{+\infty}\delta^{t}\cdot u(t).$$ The continuous time version is: $$U(u(t)_{t\in \mathbb T})=\int_{0}^{+\infty}\exp(-\rho\cdot t)\cdot u(t),$$ where $\rho=-\log(\delta)>0$.

Time Preferences

Hyperbolic Discounting

Hyperbolic Discounting is a behavioral economics discounting model which is used as an alternative to the standard model (exponential discounting). Under hyperbolic discounting $D(t)=(1+\alpha\cdot t)^{-\frac{\beta}{\alpha}}$ where $\alpha>0$, $\beta>0$.

Time Preferences

Exponential Discounting as a Particular Case of Recursive Utility

Consider the standard exponential discounting model with discrete time and finite horizon. We shall re-normalize the discounted utility by multiplying the utility by a factor of $1-\delta$, so the the discounted utility at period zero can be expressed as: $$U_{0}=(1-\delta)\sum_{t=0}^{T}\delta^{t}u_t.$$ Remember that $u_{t}$ is the instantaneous utility-- the utility that is actually received at period $t$. By the same token the discounted utility at period $\mathbf k$ can be written as: $$U_{k}=(1-\delta)\sum_{t=k}^{T}\delta^{t-k}u_t.$$

Time Preferences

Recursive Utility

Notice that we can write the discounted utility at period $\mathbf t$ in a recursive manner:

$$U_{t}=(1-\delta)u_t+\delta U_{t+1} \tag{R}$$

Using the fact that with a finite horizon, $U_{T+1}=0$ by definition, we can use the recursive equation (R) as a way of defining the discounted utility at a given period.

Time Preferences

Recursive Utility, The General Case

We saw that exponential discounting preferences can be stated in terms of recursive utility: $$U_{t}=(1-\delta)u_t+\delta U_{t+1},$$ The above formulation is a particular case of the following recursive preferences: $$U_{t}=\left((1-\delta)u_t^{\theta}+\delta U_{t+1}^{\theta}\right)^{\frac1\theta}$$ where $0<\theta\leq 1$ and $0<\delta < 1$.

Time Preferences

Recursive Utility, The General Case

Consider $\theta=\frac12$ and $\delta=\frac12$. Suppose $u_0=4$, $u_1=16$, and from $t=2$ onwards utility is constant: $u_2=u_3=\cdots=4$. Then: $$U_2=4, \quad U_1=\left(\tfrac12\sqrt{16}+\tfrac12\sqrt{4}\right)^2=9, \quad U_0=\left(\tfrac12\sqrt{4}+\tfrac12\sqrt{9}\right)^2=6.25.$$

Note: We assume that if the utility flow is constant from some period onwards, $u_t=\overline{u}$, then $U_t$ is also constant from that period onwards, $U_t=\overline{u}$. This assumption is needed because we cannot compute $U_\infty$ directly for infinite sequences.

Time Preferences

Definition: Dynamically Consistent Preferences

Consider an agent with hyperbolic preferences whose optimal choice in period $t=0$ are $(c_{0},c_{1},\ldots,c_{T})$. After the first period elapses, if the individual re-evaluates her choices at $t=1$ (which now corresponds to the present), it is possible that her optimal choice $(\hat c_{1},\ldots,\hat c_{T})$ will differ from her original optimal choices, $\hat c_{1}\neq c_{1},\ldots,\hat c_{T}\neq c_{T}$. If that happens we say the agent behavior is dynamically inconsistent.

Recursive preferences (which includes exponential discounting) are always dynamically consistent but hyperbolic discounting may (not always) generate dynamically inconsistent choices.

Dynamic Consistency

When time preferences might exhibit dynamic inconsistency behavior, we need to specify how individuals will react. There are three possible alternatives:

  1. The naive approach: the individual does not realize that he/she might change her preferences in the future. He/she makes choices in the present period ignoring that his/her plans might be foiled (by himself/herself) in the future.
  2. Strategic approach without commitment: the individual realizes that his/her future self might behave differently than his/her present-self wishes and so he/she will act expecting that her future self will react to her choices.
  3. Strategic approach with commitment: the individual realizes that his/her future self might behave differently than his/her present self wishes and so he/she will remove the ability of her future self to make choices by committing to the present choices.

Time Preferences

The Intertemporal Rate of Substitution

Assuming exponential discounting, at period $t$, what is the maximum amount the agent would be willing to sacrifice of future utility $U_{t+1}$ in order to increase the instantaneous utility $u_{t}$ by one util?

The answer is the marginal rate of intertemporal substitution of utility, MRIS. $$ (1-\delta)\Delta u_t+\delta \Delta U_{t+1}=0 \Rightarrow \mathrm{MRIS}=\left|\dfrac{ \Delta U_{t+1}}{ \Delta u_{t}}\right|=\dfrac{\delta}{1-\delta}. $$

So, in the case of exponential discounting the MRIS is a constant.

Recursive Utility and Intertemporal Rate of Substitution

In order to allow for preferences with non-constant MRIS, the following family of recursive utility functions is often used: $$U_{t}=\left((1-\delta)u_t^{\theta}+\delta U_{t+1}^{\theta}\right)^{\frac1\theta}$$ where $0<\theta\leq 1$.

$$\mathrm{MRIS}=\left|\dfrac{ \Delta U_{t+1}}{ \Delta u_{t}}\right|=\dfrac{\delta}{1-\delta}\left(\dfrac{U_{t+1}}{u_{t}}\right)^{1-\theta}. $$
